Conservation of work and energy

• Energy is that “natural” property of a system which stays the same unless it exchanges (in or out) some of this property with the environment – this means the total energy of a system can only increase or decrease if energy is gained from or lost to the environment respectively.

• There are many forms of energy, e.g. nuclear, chemical, solar, etc. – these may be transformed from one form to another. Modern technology is built on this idea, e.g. we transform chemical or nuclear energy in power plants into electrical energy, and this in turn is transformed into whatever energy is required by appliances in your home.

• The total mechanical energy of a system E, consists of two types of energy – the total energy is the sum of the kinetic (K) and stored or potential energy (U) possessed by a system: Emech = K + U. Potential energy is stored energy a system has by virtue of its position in a force field, and kinetic energy is the energy it possesses due to its motion.

• The law of conservation of energy goes way beyond Newtonian mechanics to include new ideas about heat, chemical energy, and about the individual energy of atoms and molecules (e.g. thermal energy Eth) that make up the system.

• Energy can be transformed inside a system from one form into another, e.g. gravitational potential energy can be converted to kinetic energy and vice versa. In this case the total system energy, Esys = K + U + Eth is conserved (Esys = 0).

• Energy transfer can occur between the system and the environment. The mechanical transfer of energy is called work (W), so Esys = W.
